Climate Action

Climate Action refers to long-term shifts in temperatures and weather patterns. Human activities, particularly the burning of fossil fuels like coal, oil, and gas, have been the primary driver of climate change since the 1800s. These activities release greenhouse gas emissions, such as carbon dioxide and methane, which act like a blanket around the Earth, trapping heat and raising temperatures. Other sources of these gases include using gasoline for transportation, coal for heating, clearing land, cutting down forests, and activities in the agriculture, oil, and gas sectors. Greenhouse gas emissions from energy, industry, transport, buildings, agriculture, and land use are the main contributors to climate change.



Key manifestations of climate Action



Global Warming

The average global temperature has been increasing over the past century, leading to rising temperatures across the planet. This warming trend has far-reaching consequences for ecosystems, weather patterns, sea levels, and human societies.

Changes in Precipitation Patterns

Climate change influences precipitation patterns, leading to alterations in rainfall, snowfall, and drought occurrence. Some regions may experience increased precipitation and flooding, while others face more prolonged droughts and water scarcity.

Melting Ice Caps and Glaciers

Rising temperatures cause ice caps, glaciers, and polar ice sheets to melt at accelerated rates. This melting contributes to sea-level rise, threatening coastal communities and habitats, and disrupting ocean circulation patterns.

Ocean Acidification

Increased CO2 levels in the atmosphere lead to higher levels of carbon dioxide being absorbed by the oceans. This process, known as ocean acidification, alters seawater chemistry, affecting marine ecosystems and organisms such as coral reefs, shellfish, and plankton.
